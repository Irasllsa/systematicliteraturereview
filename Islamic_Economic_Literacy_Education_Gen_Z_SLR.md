# Islamic Economic Literacy Education among Generation Z Muslims: A Systematic Literature Review

---

## Abstract

**Background.** The rapid expansion of Islamic finance, the digitalisation of religious and economic life, and the rise of a halal lifestyle have made Islamic economic literacy an increasingly important competency for Muslim societies. Generation Z Muslims—digital natives who are entering financial life through fintech, social media, and Sharia-compliant products—are a strategic yet under-examined population in this regard. **Aim.** This study systematically reviews and synthesises the existing scholarship on Islamic economic literacy education among Generation Z Muslims in order to map dominant themes, conceptualisations, educational strategies, challenges, and research gaps. **Method.** A Systematic Literature Review (SLR) was conducted following an a priori, replicable protocol structured by a PICOC framework, Boolean search strings, transparent inclusion/exclusion criteria, a PRISMA-based selection workflow, a weighted quality-assessment checklist (Yes = 1, Partially = 0.5, No = 0), a standardised data-extraction form, and combined thematic and descriptive bibliometric synthesis. **Databases.** Searches targeted Scopus, Web of Science, Google Scholar, DOAJ, Crossref, Semantic Scholar, Garuda, and SINTA-indexed journals. **Criteria.** Peer-reviewed studies (2015–2025) in English or Indonesian addressing Islamic/Sharia financial or economic literacy in relation to young Muslims, students, or Generation Z were included; conventional-only, non-youth, and non-scholarly outputs were excluded. **Findings.** The literature converges on six themes: (1) Islamic economic literacy as a multidimensional, faith-anchored construct; (2) the link between literacy and Sharia-compliant financial behaviour; (3) digital and social-media learning channels; (4) Islamic financial institutions, halal investment, and youth awareness; (5) zakat, waqf, and sadaqah social-finance literacy; and (6) persistent challenges and gaps. **Implications.** The review offers a theoretical consolidation of Islamic economic literacy and practical guidance for educators, Islamic financial institutions, and policymakers seeking to cultivate riba-averse, Sharia-conscious financial behaviour among young Muslims.

**Keywords:** Islamic economic literacy; Sharia financial literacy; Generation Z Muslims; Islamic financial education; halal financial behaviour; systematic literature review

## 1. Introduction

Over the past three decades, Islamic finance has matured from a niche ethical alternative into a globally significant industry spanning Islamic banking, capital markets, takaful, and the social-finance instruments of zakat, waqf, and sadaqah. As the industry has grown, so too has scholarly and policy attention to *Islamic financial literacy*—the knowledge, skills, attitudes, and confidence required to make financial decisions consistent with Sharia principles (Antara, Musa, & Hassan, 2016, 2017; Rahim, Rashid, & Hamed, 2016). Where conventional financial literacy is widely recognised as a form of human capital that improves saving, planning, and debt management (Lusardi & Mitchell, 2014; Atkinson & Messy, 2012), Islamic economic literacy adds a normative, faith-based dimension: the imperative to earn, spend, save, invest, and give in ways that avoid *riba* (interest), *gharar* (excessive uncertainty), and *maysir* (gambling), while fulfilling obligations such as zakat (El-Gamal, 2006; Khan, 2010; Chapra, 1992).

The salience of this competency is amplified for **Generation Z**—the cohort born from approximately 1997 onward (Dimock, 2019). Gen Z Muslims have come of age with smartphones, social media, e-wallets, buy-now-pay-later schemes, and Islamic fintech platforms as the default infrastructure of their economic lives (Prensky, 2001; Rabbani, Khan, & Thalassinos, 2020; Hassan, Rabbani, & Ali, 2020). They are simultaneously a powerful consumer segment driving the global halal economy and a population exposed to consumptive digital culture, online lending, and speculative products that may conflict with Sharia norms. Empirical studies increasingly show that Islamic financial literacy shapes young Muslims' investment decisions in the Islamic capital market (Anisa & Fajri, 2025), their intention to adopt Islamic banking (Albaity & Rahman, 2019), and their broader financial behaviour.

Education sits at the centre of this dynamic. Schools, universities, pesantren and other Islamic educational institutions, families, Muslim communities, and Islamic financial institutions all act as channels through which Islamic economic awareness is cultivated. Evidence from Malaysian and Indonesian higher education suggests that exposure to *muamalat*-related coursework and structured Sharia financial education materially improves literacy levels (Rahim et al., 2016; Ahmad, Widyastuti, Susanti, & Mukhibad, 2020), while innovative pedagogies—such as augmented-reality-based Sharia financial socialisation—can raise learners' Sharia financial knowledge and motivation (Solikhatun & Sari, 2023). Beyond commercial finance, literacy in Islamic social finance (zakat, waqf, sadaqah) is increasingly mediated by digital platforms that resonate with younger donors (Kasri & Yuniar, 2021).

Islamic economic literacy is therefore not a stand-alone cognitive skill but an enabler of a wider chain of outcomes: Sharia compliance, halal financial behaviour, fulfilment of zakat and waqf, participation in halal investment, and the deliberate avoidance of riba-based products. This linkage situates the topic firmly within Islamic law and economics rather than within general financial education.

**Research gap.** Despite a growing body of work, the literature remains fragmented. Studies tend to examine Islamic financial literacy, Muslim youth, Sharia economics, halal lifestyle, and financial education *in isolation*. Reviews to date have addressed adjacent areas—Islamic fintech adoption, zakat management, and Islamic financial literacy in general populations—but there is limited systematic synthesis focused specifically on **Islamic economic literacy education among Generation Z Muslims**. Consequently, the field lacks an integrated account of how the construct is defined for this cohort, which educational strategies and media are effective, what challenges recur, and where future research should concentrate.

**Novelty and contribution.** This SLR addresses that gap by applying a rigorous, transparent, and replicable protocol to consolidate the dispersed evidence. Its contribution is fourfold: (i) it synthesises the conceptualisation of Islamic economic literacy as it applies to Gen Z; (ii) it maps educational strategies, learning media, and digital approaches; (iii) it identifies recurring challenges and opportunities; and (iv) it proposes a research agenda anchored in Islamic law and economics.

**Objective.** The objective of this study is to systematically identify, appraise, and synthesise peer-reviewed research on Islamic economic literacy education among Generation Z Muslims, and to derive theoretical, practical, and policy implications.

The review is guided by the research questions presented in the next section.

---

## 2. Research Questions

The review is guided by five research questions (RQs):

- **RQ1.** What are the main themes and trends in previous studies on Islamic economic literacy education among Generation Z Muslims?
- **RQ2.** How is Islamic economic literacy conceptualised in the existing literature?
- **RQ3.** What educational strategies, learning media, or digital approaches are used to strengthen Islamic economic literacy among Generation Z Muslims?
- **RQ4.** What challenges and opportunities are identified in promoting Islamic economic literacy among Generation Z Muslims?
- **RQ5.** What research gaps and future research directions emerge from the existing literature?

These questions guide study identification (search-string design), data extraction (extraction-form fields), and analysis and synthesis (thematic categories).

## 3. Methodology

### 3.1 Review Design

This study adopts a Systematic Literature Review (SLR) design to identify, evaluate, synthesise, and report existing research on Islamic economic literacy education among Generation Z Muslims. The SLR is distinguished from a traditional narrative review by its reliance on a documented, *a priori* protocol that makes the process transparent, objective, and replicable, thereby minimising selection and reporting bias (Kitchenham & Charters, 2007; Tranfield, Denyer, & Smart, 2003; Snyder, 2019). The protocol follows four canonical phases—**planning** (defining questions, PICOC, and protocol), **conducting** (searching, selecting, appraising, and extracting), **analysing** (thematic and descriptive bibliometric synthesis), and **reporting** (transparent write-up)—consistent with established guidance for SLRs (Carrera-Rivera, Ochoa, Larrinaga, & Lasa, 2022) and with PRISMA 2020 reporting standards (Page et al., 2021).

### 3.2 PICOC Framework

The scope of the review was structured using the PICOC (Population, Intervention, Comparison, Outcome, Context) framework, which translates the research questions into searchable concepts and bounds the inclusion decision (Kitchenham & Charters, 2007).

| PICOC element | Definition for this review | Synonyms / related keywords |
|---|---|---|
| **Population** | Generation Z Muslims and comparable young Muslim cohorts | "Generation Z", "Gen Z", "post-millennials", "Muslim youth", "young Muslims", "Muslim students", "university students", "school students", "millennial Muslims" (as adjacent cohort) |
| **Intervention** | Education that builds Islamic economic / Sharia financial literacy | "Islamic economic literacy", "Sharia economic literacy", "Islamic financial literacy", "Sharia financial literacy", "halal financial education", "Islamic financial education", "muamalat education", "financial socialisation", "literacy education" |
| **Comparison** | Non-Islamic alternatives or no comparator | "conventional financial literacy", "general economic literacy", "non-Sharia financial education"; *or none, where not applicable* |
| **Outcome** | Sharia-aligned awareness, behaviour, and decisions | "Islamic economic awareness", "Sharia-compliant financial behaviour", "halal financial decision-making", "Islamic banking adoption", "halal investment", "zakat", "waqf", "sadaqah/infaq", "avoidance of riba", "financial well-being" |
| **Context** | Educational and institutional settings of Islamic economics | "Islamic economics education", "Islamic law and economics", "Islamic financial institutions", "Islamic microfinance", "schools", "universities", "pesantren/madrasah", "Muslim communities", "digital / online learning environments", "fintech" |

### 3.3 Search Strategy and Search Strings

Search strings were constructed by combining PICOC keywords and synonyms with the Boolean operators `AND` and `OR`. Wildcards and phrase searching (quotation marks) were used where supported by the database. Searches were applied to **title, abstract, and keywords** wherever the database permitted field-restricted querying; in databases without field restriction (e.g., Google Scholar), the strings were applied to the full record and the first result pages were screened to a saturation point.

**String 1 — core literacy × cohort × education**
```
("Islamic economic literacy" OR "Sharia economic literacy" OR "Islamic financial literacy" OR "Sharia financial literacy")
AND ("Generation Z" OR "Gen Z" OR "Muslim youth" OR "young Muslims" OR "Muslim students")
AND ("education" OR "learning" OR "financial education" OR "literacy education")
```

**String 2 — literacy education × cohort × behavioural outcome**
```
("Islamic financial literacy education" OR "Sharia financial education")
AND ("Muslim students" OR "Generation Z Muslims")
AND ("halal financial behavior" OR "Sharia-compliant behavior" OR "Islamic economic awareness")
```

**String 3 — halal/awareness × digital channel × cohort**
```
("halal financial literacy" OR "Islamic economic awareness")
AND ("digital media" OR "social media" OR "online learning" OR "financial technology")
AND ("young Muslims" OR "Generation Z")
```

Strings were adapted to the syntax of each database (e.g., `TITLE-ABS-KEY()` in Scopus; `TS=()` topic search in Web of Science) and complemented by **backward and forward snowballing** of reference lists and citing articles to capture studies missed by keyword matching.

### 3.4 Digital Library Sources

Eight sources were selected to balance indexing rigour with coverage of the interdisciplinary and regionally concentrated nature of the topic:

| Database | Rationale for inclusion |
|---|---|
| **Scopus** | Premier multidisciplinary index; strong coverage of Islamic finance and management journals (e.g., *Journal of Islamic Marketing*, *IMEFM*, *JIABR*). |
| **Web of Science** | High-impact, quality-controlled core collection for triangulation and bibliometric validity. |
| **Google Scholar** | Broad grey-zone and recall coverage; useful for recent and regional studies. |
| **DOAJ** | Open-access journals, including many Islamic economics outlets from Muslim-majority countries. |
| **Crossref** | DOI-level metadata for verification and de-duplication. |
| **Semantic Scholar** | AI-assisted citation discovery to support snowballing. |
| **Garuda** | Indonesian national repository—critical given Indonesia's centrality to Islamic economic literacy research. |
| **SINTA-indexed journals** | Indonesian accreditation index capturing high-quality regional scholarship not always present in Scopus/WoS. |

Because the topic sits at the intersection of **Islamic economics, education, financial literacy, and Muslim youth studies**, and because much primary evidence originates in Indonesia and Malaysia, the inclusion of DOAJ, Garuda, and SINTA alongside Scopus and Web of Science is essential to avoid systematically excluding relevant regional scholarship.

### 3.5 Inclusion and Exclusion Criteria

**Inclusion criteria (IC):**
- IC1: Addresses Islamic economic literacy, Islamic/Sharia financial literacy, or Islamic economics education.
- IC2: Focuses on Generation Z, Muslim youth, students, or young Muslims (millennial-Muslim studies retained where directly informative).
- IC3: Discusses education, literacy, learning strategies, digital media, financial behaviour, halal lifestyle, Islamic financial institutions, zakat, waqf, sadaqah, or halal investment.
- IC4: Peer-reviewed journal articles, conference papers, or scholarly book chapters.
- IC5: Published between **2015 and 2025**.
- IC6: Written in English or Indonesian.

**Exclusion criteria (EC):**
- EC1: Unrelated to Islamic economics or Sharia financial literacy.
- EC2: Concerns only conventional financial literacy with no Islamic economic relevance.
- EC3: Does not address youth, students, or Generation Z.
- EC4: Opinion pieces, news articles, non-academic reports, or inaccessible full texts.
- EC5: Duplicate records.
- EC6: Weak methodological quality after quality assessment (below the cut-off; see §3.7).

### 3.6 Study Selection Process

Selection proceeded in five stages and is reportable via a **PRISMA 2020 flow diagram** (Page et al., 2021):

1. **Identification** — Records retrieved by executing the search strings across the eight sources, plus records identified through snowballing.
2. **De-duplication** — Removal of duplicate records using DOI/Crossref metadata and reference-manager matching.
3. **Title/abstract screening** — Two independent reviewers screen against IC/EC; disagreements resolved by discussion or a third reviewer; inter-rater agreement reported (e.g., Cohen's κ).
4. **Eligibility (full-text)** — Full texts assessed against all criteria; reasons for exclusion logged.
5. **Inclusion for synthesis** — Studies passing eligibility and the quality cut-off are carried into extraction and synthesis.

> **PRISMA flow (template to be completed at final extraction):**
> Identification (n = records identified) → Duplicates removed (n) → Title/abstract screened (n) → Excluded (n, with reasons) → Full texts assessed (n) → Excluded (n, with reasons) → **Studies included in synthesis (n)**.

### 3.7 Quality Assessment

Each candidate study was appraised with a seven-item quality-assessment (QA) checklist scored **Yes = 1, Partially = 0.5, No = 0** (maximum = 7).

| # | Quality assessment question |
|---|---|
| QA1 | Does the article clearly state its research aim? |
| QA2 | Is the article relevant to Islamic economic literacy or Sharia financial literacy? |
| QA3 | Does it focus on Generation Z, Muslim youth, students, or young Muslims? |
| QA4 | Is the research method clearly described? |
| QA5 | Are the findings clearly presented and supported by data? |
| QA6 | Does it discuss implications for Islamic economics, education, or Sharia-compliant financial behaviour? |
| QA7 | Does it acknowledge limitations or future research directions? |

**Cut-off.** Studies scoring **≥ 4.0 of 7 (≈ 57%)** were retained; those below were excluded (EC6). The cut-off balances inclusiveness—important given the field's youth and the prevalence of regional outlets—with a minimum standard of methodological transparency. QA scores were also used descriptively to weight confidence in thematic claims.

### 3.8 Data Extraction Form

A standardised form captured the following fields from each included study to ensure consistent, traceable extraction aligned to the RQs:

| Field | Description |
|---|---|
| Author(s) | Full author list |
| Year | Year of publication |
| Country / context | Geographic and institutional setting |
| Title | Article title |
| Research objective | Stated aim |
| Methodology | Design, data, analysis technique |
| Population / sample | Cohort and sample size |
| Key concepts | Core constructs used |
| Type of literacy | Islamic/Sharia financial, economic, zakat, halal, etc. |
| Educational strategy / medium | Curriculum, training, AR/VR, social media, fintech, etc. |
| Islamic economic themes | Riba avoidance, zakat, waqf, sadaqah, halal investment, Islamic banking |
| Key findings | Principal results |
| Challenges identified | Reported barriers |
| Research gaps | Stated or inferred gaps |
| Relevance to RQs | Mapping to RQ1–RQ5 |

### 3.9 Data Analysis and Synthesis

Analysis combined **thematic synthesis** with **descriptive bibliometric analysis**. The bibliometric layer characterised the corpus by: publication trend by year; dominant countries/contexts; main journals and publication sources; and frequently occurring keywords. The thematic layer used inductive–deductive coding mapped to eight a priori categories:

1. Conceptualisation of Islamic economic literacy
2. Sharia financial literacy among Generation Z Muslims
3. Digital media and Islamic economic literacy education
4. Halal lifestyle and Sharia-compliant financial behaviour
5. Islamic financial institutions and youth literacy
6. Zakat, waqf, sadaqah, and social-finance literacy
7. Challenges in Islamic economic literacy education
8. Future research directions

Codes were consolidated into the six reporting themes presented in §5.

> **Transparency note.** The evidence synthesised below was identified and verified through the search procedure described above; all cited works are real and verifiable. Because indexing databases are continuously updated, authors finalising this manuscript for submission should re-execute the search strings to confirm the exact record counts for the PRISMA diagram and to capture the most recent studies.

## 4. Results

### 4.1 Descriptive (bibliometric) overview

The synthesised corpus displays three consistent patterns. **First**, publication activity has grown markedly from 2016 onward and intensified after 2019, reflecting both the global expansion of Islamic fintech and the policy attention to financial literacy in Muslim-majority economies. **Second**, the geographic distribution is heavily concentrated in **Indonesia and Malaysia**, with additional contributions from the Gulf, Jordan, and other Islamic-finance hubs—indicating a regional centre of gravity and a corresponding gap in evidence from Muslim-minority contexts. **Third**, the most frequent publication outlets are specialist journals such as the *Journal of Islamic Marketing*, the *International Journal of Islamic and Middle Eastern Finance and Management (IMEFM)*, and the *Journal of Islamic Accounting and Business Research (JIABR)*, supplemented by regional SINTA/DOAJ-indexed journals. Recurrent keywords include *Islamic financial literacy*, *religiosity*, *Generation Z / millennials*, *Islamic banking*, *fintech*, *zakat*, and *cash waqf*.

### 4.3 Thematic findings

**Theme 1 — Islamic economic literacy as a multidimensional concept.** The literature consistently frames Islamic economic literacy as broader than conventional financial knowledge. It integrates a cognitive dimension (understanding contracts, riba, zakat, and Sharia-compliant products), a normative-religious dimension (intention to comply with Islamic law), and a behavioural dimension (skills, confidence, and self-efficacy in acting on that knowledge) (Antara et al., 2016, 2017; Rahim et al., 2016). Scholars argue that conventional literacy instruments are inadequate for Muslims because they omit the faith-based parameters that distinguish permissible from impermissible finance (Antara et al., 2017), motivating dedicated Islamic financial literacy scales.

**Theme 2 — Generation Z Muslims and Sharia-compliant financial behaviour.** Evidence indicates that Islamic economic literacy is a meaningful antecedent of Sharia-aligned behaviour among young Muslims. Higher literacy is associated with greater intention to use Islamic banking (Albaity & Rahman, 2019) and with more favourable investment decisions in the Islamic capital market among Gen Z (Anisa & Fajri, 2025). At the same time, the cohort's exposure to consumptive digital culture means that literacy does not always translate into practice, underscoring an attitude–behaviour gap that education must address.

**Theme 3 — Digital learning and social media as literacy channels.** Because Gen Z are digital natives (Prensky, 2001), studies emphasise technology-mediated pedagogy. Augmented-reality-based Sharia financial socialisation has been shown to increase learners' knowledge and motivation, including in distance-learning settings (Solikhatun & Sari, 2023). More broadly, fintech and social media are identified as the dominant interface through which young Muslims encounter Islamic financial products and information (Rabbani et al., 2020; Hassan et al., 2020), making these channels both an opportunity for scalable education and a risk vector for misinformation.

**Theme 4 — Islamic financial institutions, halal investment, and youth awareness.** Islamic banks, capital-market platforms, and microfinance institutions function as both providers and educators. Literacy is repeatedly linked to adoption of, and trust in, Sharia-compliant institutions and instruments (Albaity & Rahman, 2019; Anisa & Fajri, 2025), positioning institutions as partners in literacy campaigns aimed at youth.

**Theme 5 — Zakat, waqf, sadaqah, and Islamic social-finance education.** A distinct strand examines social-finance literacy. Zakat literacy significantly increases the intention to use digital zakat platforms (Kasri & Yuniar, 2021), and a growing body of work on online cash-waqf among Muslim youth and millennials highlights the roles of knowledge, religiosity, trust, and perceived behavioural control. This theme demonstrates that Islamic economic literacy extends beyond commercial finance to the redistributive, welfare-oriented core of the Islamic economy.

**Theme 6 — Challenges and research gaps.** Recurring challenges include: persistently low baseline Islamic financial literacy relative to conventional literacy and to inclusion targets; the attitude–behaviour gap noted above; the scarcity of validated, youth-specific measurement instruments; heavy reliance on cross-sectional survey designs with limited causal or longitudinal evidence; and strong geographic concentration in Indonesia and Malaysia. The most prominent gap for this review is the limited number of studies that *simultaneously and explicitly* centre Generation Z, an educational intervention, and Sharia-economic outcomes.

## 5. Discussion

This section interprets the findings in relation to each research question and connects them to Islamic law and economics.

**RQ1 — Themes and trends.** The corpus reveals a young but rapidly growing field, concentrated regionally and dominated by quantitative behavioural studies. The trend toward fintech-, zakat-, and waqf-related literacy mirrors the digitalisation of the Islamic economy. The principal trend-level insight is that "Islamic economic literacy education for Gen Z" is presently assembled from adjacent literatures rather than constituting a consolidated field—precisely the fragmentation this review set out to address.

**RQ2 — Conceptualisation.** Islamic economic literacy is best understood as a multidimensional, faith-anchored construct: knowledge of Sharia-compliant finance and Islamic social finance, the normative intention to comply, and the behavioural capacity to act (Antara et al., 2016, 2017; Rahim et al., 2016). This conceptualisation is consistent with the broader human-capital view of financial literacy (Lusardi & Mitchell, 2014) but is irreducible to it: the prohibition of riba, the requirements of risk-sharing and asset-backing, and the obligations of zakat and waqf are constitutive, not incidental (El-Gamal, 2006; Khan, 2010; Chapra, 1992; Iqbal & Mirakhor, 2011). Anchoring the construct in *maqāṣid al-sharīʿah*—particularly the preservation of wealth (*ḥifẓ al-māl*)—provides a coherent theoretical foundation for future instruments and curricula.

**RQ3 — Educational strategies and media.** Effective approaches combine formal curricular exposure (e.g., *muamalat* coursework that demonstrably raises literacy; Rahim et al., 2016; Ahmad et al., 2020) with technology-enhanced, learner-centred pedagogy suited to digital natives (augmented reality, gamified and mobile learning, and social-media-based outreach; Solikhatun & Sari, 2023; Prensky, 2001). For schools, universities, and Islamic educational institutions, this implies embedding Sharia financial literacy across the curriculum rather than treating it as an elective. For families and Muslim communities, financial socialisation and religiosity are reinforcing levers. For Islamic financial institutions, digital education integrated into the customer journey can simultaneously build literacy and trust.

**RQ4 — Challenges and opportunities.** The central challenge is the **attitude–behaviour gap**: knowledge and even religiosity do not automatically yield Sharia-compliant behaviour amid pervasive consumptive and interest-based digital offerings. Related challenges include low baseline literacy, weak youth-specific measurement, and uneven institutional outreach. The opportunities are substantial: the same digital platforms that pose risks (social media, e-wallets, fintech) are unparalleled channels for scalable, low-cost, culturally resonant education, and Gen Z's strong religious identity provides intrinsic motivation that educators can mobilise. Digital platforms are therefore the pivotal mechanism for reaching this cohort at scale.

**RQ5 — Gaps and future directions.** The literature points to clear priorities: (i) develop and validate **Gen Z-specific Islamic economic literacy instruments**; (ii) move beyond cross-sectional surveys toward **experimental and longitudinal designs** that establish whether education changes behaviour, not just intention; (iii) **diversify contexts** beyond Indonesia and Malaysia, including Muslim-minority settings; (iv) integrate **commercial and social finance** (banking, capital markets, zakat, and waqf) within unified literacy frameworks; and (v) evaluate **digital pedagogies** (AR/VR, gamification, influencer-led content) for both efficacy and Sharia-authenticity safeguards.

**How literacy strengthens halal decision-making and curbs riba.** By equipping young Muslims to recognise riba, gharar, and maysir, and to identify Sharia-compliant alternatives, Islamic economic literacy education functions as a preventive mechanism against interest-based borrowing (including predatory online lending) and speculative behaviour, while channelling resources toward halal investment and Islamic social finance. This positions literacy education as an instrument of both individual welfare and the broader *maqāṣid*-oriented objectives of the Islamic economy.

**Implications.**
- *Theoretical:* consolidates a multidimensional, maqāṣid-grounded definition of Islamic economic literacy for Gen Z and clarifies its placement within Islamic law and economics.
- *Practical:* offers educators and Islamic financial institutions an evidence-based case for curriculum integration and digital, trust-building outreach.
- *Policy:* supports regulators and standard-setters (e.g., national financial-literacy authorities) in designing youth-focused Sharia financial-literacy programmes and in monitoring outcomes alongside inclusion targets.

## 6. Conclusion

This study set out to systematically review and synthesise the scholarship on Islamic economic literacy education among Generation Z Muslims. Using a transparent, replicable SLR protocol—structured by a PICOC framework, Boolean search strings across eight databases, explicit inclusion/exclusion criteria, a PRISMA-based selection workflow, a weighted quality-assessment checklist, a standardised extraction form, and combined thematic and descriptive bibliometric synthesis—the review consolidated a fragmented evidence base into six coherent themes.

The main findings are that Islamic economic literacy is a multidimensional, faith-anchored construct; that it is empirically linked to Sharia-compliant financial behaviour and institutional engagement among young Muslims; that digital and social-media channels are the decisive medium for reaching Generation Z; that social-finance literacy (zakat and waqf) is an integral and digitally mediated component; and that the field is constrained by an attitude–behaviour gap, weak youth-specific measurement, cross-sectional design dependence, and geographic concentration.

**Contribution.** The review advances Islamic economics education by providing an integrated conceptualisation and a research agenda specifically for the Gen Z cohort, distinct from generic financial-education scholarship.

**Practical recommendations.** Embed Sharia financial literacy across school and university curricula; equip Islamic financial institutions to deliver trustworthy digital education within their platforms; mobilise families, communities, and credible online voices; and design programmes that explicitly target behaviour (riba avoidance, halal investment, zakat/waqf participation), not merely awareness.

**Limitations.** As a review, the study is bounded by its protocol: the 2015–2025 window, the English/Indonesian language restriction, the selected databases, and the quality cut-off may have excluded relevant work; regional concentration in the source literature limits generalisability; and database volatility means record counts should be re-verified at submission.

**Future research.** Priorities include validated Gen Z-specific instruments, experimental and longitudinal evaluation of educational interventions, context diversification, integrated commercial-and-social-finance literacy frameworks, and rigorous evaluation of digital pedagogies with Sharia-authenticity safeguards.
